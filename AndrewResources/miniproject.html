DAE Mini-Project

Overview

In this miniproject, you will get the opportunity to carry out a data science exercise from start to finish, applying the concepts and tools you have gained on a real-world dataset. The theme of this project is the social and economic wellness of our nation. Your job is to develop a research question \ topic which examines the relationships between some response variable and a range of predictive variables, carry out the relevant analysis, interpret and communicate your findings, using local public datasets (such as StatsSA ). Examples of topics include (but not limited to): changes to distributions in crime, effects of corruption, changing healthcare needs, analysis of the demographics of the workforce, etc. We will be focusing on interpretable models to understand relationships between variables. Problems that deal with evaluating or improving predictive performance are prohibited, unless they are used as part of understanding the data-generating process.

This project will teach you how to work in groups on a data analysis project as well as some of the "softer" aspects of data analysis, which includes your ability to form a compelling research question, to conduct reproducible research, and to communicate your findings effectively. Your project should be presented such that a colleague can easily follow the arguments and calculations to reproduce the same results.

Asking the Question

One of the most time consuming parts of the project is settling on an interesting question, which can easily take between 15-20 hours. As discussed in the course, the data science process is iterative and nonlinear, and so you may have to traverse the entire process (more than once) before settling on a final question \ topic. It is best to start as early possible.

It is worth emphasising that an interesting question should not be answerable by basic plots and statistics, eg. plotting the level of some variable over time, or calculating proportions over different groups. Types of questions that you can ask, and the characteristics of good questions will be covered in class. For more information, see the relevant sections in the books Excutive Data Science and The Art of Data Science.

It is best to start your search for a research question with a broadly defined investigation such as "an investigation into factors that explain (something)", and then identify more specific research questions.

Examples of specific research questions include (but not limited to):

  • The following explanation for some effect has been reported in (some resource). We test this relationship, and examine if it changes over time.
  • The relationship between (variables) is argued to be (some way). We examine how this relationship is affected by the presence of (new variables)
  • The distribution of the response variable appears to show different groups.
    • Can the groupings be caused by (list of variables)?
    • OR, Can we find a set of variables which explains this grouping?
  • An analysis of interactions in datasets to explain (some variable)

Reproducible Research

A reproducible set of research means that another person who conducts the same analyses on the same dataset by following your methodology will reach the same results as you did. For your research to be reproducible, you will need to publish your software and the dataset that you used. Code needs to be clearly annotated and written for readability rather than efficiency. Reproducibility is usually mentioned along with replicability. Replicability is more of a statistical concept and refers to results that can be verified on independent datasets. In contrast, non-replicable results arise from data-dredging (eg. overfitting) and are specific to the dataset analysed.
